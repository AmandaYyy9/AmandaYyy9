Alzheimer's Disease

[ˈælzˌhaɪmərz dɪˈziz] n.



Alzheimer's is the most common cause of dementia, a general term for memory loss and other cognitive abilities serious enough to interfere with daily life.

The greatest known risk factor is increasing age, and the majority of people with Alzheimer's are 65 and older. But Alzheimer's is not just a disease of old age. Approximately 200,000 Americans under the age of 65 have younger-onset Alzheimer’s disease (also known as early-onset Alzheimer’s).

7 Stages

1  No Impairment
At this stage, Alzheimer’s disease is undetectable. This stage is also sometimes called No Cognitive Decline.
2  Very Mild Cognitive Decline
Patients may forget where they left their car keys or their purse. These symptoms are typically not noticed by the individual’s family members or physician.
3  Mild Cognitive Decline
Patients experience increased forgetfulness as well as slight difficulty with focus or concentration.
4  Moderate Cognitive Decline
Stage four comprises what is clinically described as early-stage dementia. Patients will experience increased forgetfulness, often forgetting recent events.
5  Moderately Severe Cognitive Decline
Major memory deficiencies are present beginning in stage five, and patients may require assistance with activities of daily living, such as bathing and dressing.
6  Severe Cognitive Decline
Significant personality changes may also be noticeable at this stage, as individuals may suffer from delusions, anxiety, or agitation.
7  Very Severe Cognitive Decline
Most people will have lost their ability to communicate. They often require assistance with most of their activities, including toileting, eating, and other daily activities.

What happens?

Among tau’s multiple functions in healthy brain cells, a very important one is stabilization of the internal microtubules that cells use to distribute nutrients and other important substances from one part of a nerve cell (neuron) to another. Alzheimer’s disease is well known to feature neurofibrillary tangles that are composed of modified tau protein. The total amount of abnormal tau in the AD brain is linked to disease stage and severity.
